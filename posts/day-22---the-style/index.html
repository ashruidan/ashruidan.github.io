Day 22 - The Style

Dec 22, 2024

Introduction

In Go, your style is the personal touch you bring to the game. It reflects your preferences, strengths, and approach to solving problems on the board. At lower levels, you might not be able to discern your style. Only with experience and exposure to different ways of playing will you start noticing patterns that define your unique approach to the game.

Balance of Power

One of the easiest ways to define your style is by understanding your inclination toward the balance between power and territory :

  • Influence-Focused Style:
    You prioritize gaining thickness in multiple areas of the board to control large regions. This style values long-term potential over immediate gains.

  • Territory-Focused Style:
    You aim to secure points quickly and efficiently, starting with the corners and then extending to the sides.

Most players fall somewhere between these two extremes, adapting their play based on the situation.

Aggressiveness

Another way to distinguish your style is by examining your approach to conflict :

  • Fighting Style:
    You actively seek complications, aiming for kills and engaging in tactical battles to weaken or capture opponent groups.

  • Building Style:
    You focus on creating the largest and most stable areas possible, avoiding conflict until absolutely necessary.

Your aggressiveness will depend on your reading skills, positional judgment, and comfort in high-pressure situations.

Orthodoxy

This characteristic reflects how closely you adhere to the principles, theories, and proverbs of Go:

  • Orthodox Style:
    You follow established principles and theories of Go with precision, relying on proven strategies and shapes.

  • Unorthodox Style:
    You trust your intuition, making moves that might defy conventional wisdom but feel right to you.

Balancing orthodoxy and intuition can help you adapt your playstyle and keep opponents guessing.

Proverb of the Day

“You Can Play Go But Don’t Let Go Play You.”

This proverb encourages players to make moves that align with their style and instincts. Trusting your own judgment, rather than rigidly adhering to external expectations, creates synergy and harmony in your play.

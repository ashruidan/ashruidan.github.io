Chapter 18: The Capturing Race

Introduction

In the world of Go, life and death situations often boil down to a capturing race (semeai in japanese), where two groups compete to surround and capture each other. Mastering the capturing race is essential for determining which groups can survive and which must perish. It involves counting liberties, planning efficient moves, and understanding shared life situations like seki.

Understanding the Capturing Race

A capturing race occurs when at least two opposing groups of stones are locked in a struggle for survival. The winner is the group that captures the other by filling its liberties while keeping its own intact.

To win a capturing race, every move must maximize your liberties or reduce your opponent’s. Avoid wasted moves that don’t contribute directly to survival or capture.

In this example, we have a capturing race between triangle group and square group. Each having 3 liberties, but even if Black start to play, White will win the capturing race.

The following sequence is starting with Black :

  • Black A18, to deny the eye in A19
  • White D19, to keep the liberty count
  • Black A17, to make possible the move A19 for Black else it is a self atari
  • White F19, to put the square group in atari
  • Black A19, to put the triangle group in atari
  • White E19, capturing the square group and winning the capturing race

Seki

Not all capturing races end with one group being captured. In some cases, neither player can afford to fill the other’s liberties without jeopardizing their own group. This unique situation is called seki, where both groups share life without fully securing it.

In seki, both groups lack the two eyes needed for guaranteed life, yet neither can be captured. The shared liberties between them create a delicate balance that neither side can disrupt without losing their stones. As a result, the area involved in seki becomes neutral on the board, and no points are awarded for the shared liberties.

In this diagram, we can see that both player share 2 liberties (triangle). The first to play there lose, so we get a seki where nobody can kill the other group.

Proverb of the Day

“Eyes win semeais”

This proverb highlights the critical role of eye space in capturing races (semeais). Groups with two eyes cannot be captured, no matter how many liberties the opponent has. Securing eyes is often the deciding factor in life and death struggles.

Day 24 - The Mistake

Dec 24, 2024

Introduction

In the chapter on reviewing, we highlighted how mistakes play a role in improvement. Though unavoidable, mistakes are valuable opportunities to learn and grow. Recognizing the different types of mistakes you can make during a game helps you understand their causes and take steps to avoid them in the future.

Reading Mistakes

Reading mistakes occur when your mental visualization of future sequences is inaccurate or too shallow. You might miss a critical move or fail to account for your opponent’s best responses. To improve, you need to practice life and death problems and learning about patterns to simplify your reading.

Lack of Knowledge

Mistakes stemming from lack of knowledge happen when you encounter unfamiliar patterns or situations, such as specific joseki, tesuji, or endgame techniques. To improve, you must venture into unfamiliar territory and learn from others by observing and understanding the strategies and techniques they use that are new to you.

Direction Mistakes

Direction mistakes involve playing moves that fail to align with the overall flow of the game. They may result in inefficient positions or missed opportunities to expand. To improve, you need to regularly assess the board’s current situation and understand what it reveals about the game’s dynamics.

Mental Mistakes

Mental mistakes are rooted in psychological factors, such as overconfidence, impatience, or frustration. They can cloud your judgment and lead to hasty or poorly considered moves. You have to maintain composure, even during byo-yomi, to improve.

Proverb of the Day

“People in glass houses shouldn’t throw stones.”

This proverb reminds us that attacking from a weak position is a common mistake in Go. When your groups are not secure, attacking can backfire and make your position worse. It’s better to focus on strengthening your own groups or solidifying your territory first. Once your position is stable, your attacks will be more effective and less likely to fail.
